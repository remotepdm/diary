Diary of a remote PdM

/ Technical debt

16 Sep 2014

I’ve been thinking a lot about technical debt and the reliability of our systems ever since my lead engineer told me last week that he thought the situation was basically untenable. At first, I tried to chip away at it by allocating a certain number of story points in each sprint to technical debt tickets, and this seemed to be ok for a while, but recently we’ve been having a lot of NFS and permissions errors that are completely out of our control and I think it’s caused people to start looking around at what else is wrong.

So my lead engineer asked for a whole sprint devoted to working on reliability. Of course I took him seriously, he knows what he’s talking about, but my gut reaction was to treat him like any other stakeholder in the project and prioritise his needs against everyone else’s. This made sense on one level, but I wasn’t happy at the thought of frustrated engineers wasting time with unreliable systems either.

Looking at it again today, though, I could completely see his point of view, this was wasting man hours every single day, or at least he believed that it was. I think that’s an important point, even if it doesn’t turn out to be the case. If you think you’re spending a bunch of time being unproductive that’s just going to stress you out. I read this article on the train home this evening, and it had a line that happened to resonate:

The software production process has several major “inventory” accumulation points, itself. Stuff accumulates at those points and ends up wasting a lot of time and money.

This got me thinking some more about how we could actually measure the impact of work on reliability tickets.

The engineers get thousands of error emails every day, basically rendering the messages useless, so that’s a metric we could immediately work on, try to decrease the number of those. But to what end? It’s not actually a very useful metric for measuring anything other than quality of life, which is obviously highly subjective. What about something more radical, what about saying: “If we really believe that unreliable systems are wasting us man hours, if we work on some of these tickets, then we should see the amount of points we can clear in a sprint increase.” That feels like it’s actually measuring something important, so I’ll bring that up tomorrow and see how it goes down.

Prioritising the tickets is another kettle of fish, though, and it’s really difficult for me to do if I don’t understand how much pain individual problems are causing. We’ve got three separate groupings of these tickets at the moment (“Reliability”, “Technical Debt” and “Back-end Improvements”) and I’m not sure anyone has a crystal clear notion of a distinction between them, let alone a good general understanding of their content and which ones are top priority. I’m trying to convince my lead engineer to sit down and walk me through them, but he’s understandably reticent about another meeting, especially so early in his West Coast day. I don’t want to disrupt him or waste his time, but I think I need to persevere with this one.
